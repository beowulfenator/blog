I've always meant to get my hands on a good scrub plane. Sadly, Stanley #40 and the like have high collectible value, and therefore cost an arm and a leg on ebay. There's always the option of repurposing a Stanley #78 (and I might to that in the future), but for now I've bought some rather cheap soviet hardware that looks promising.

<!--more-->

### General Thoughts

I actually have two soviet-made scrub planes, a fairly modern "new old stock" unit from the 90s and an older one from probably the 70s.

!["Scrub plane from the 90s"](/assets/images/2022-04-28-scrub-plane-makeover/scrub-plane-1.jpg)

!["Scrub plane from the 70s"](/assets/images/2022-04-28-scrub-plane-makeover/scrub-plane-2.jpg)

The focus of this article is the second plane, which is older and heavier, and has bakelite handles. Its iron is also significantly better.

### Materials

I have a nice apple board that I bought specifically for making handles. The board is rather warped, and seems to have been dried too quickly, but it looks nice and is hard enough for handles. I've read that apple wood is the traditional material for saw handles. That's what I bought it for, originally, but I'm sure it will do nicely for hand plane knobs and totes.


### The Tote

This actually started as the tote for my Stanley #6. I downloaded [the template from Lee Valley](https://assets.leevalley.com/Original/10090/56664-stanley-number-5-and-up-plane-tote-c-06-e.pdf), and started cutting. Unfortunately, I took off too much at the bottom of the tote, so I decided to salvage it by adapting it to this plane instead. The angle of the hole is different, by the way. Stanley comes with a 63&deg; hole, and this plane has a 70&deg; hole. The handle therefore does not perfectly fit this plane, but it will do for now.

I need a scrub plane to prepare the boards, and this tote is much better than the original. In a sense, I'm eating my own dog food here, preparing the blank with the same plane that I'm refurbishing.
